# Chapter 10: Cruelty as Performance

The stage is ancient, yet the script remains terrifyingly familiar. We begin, perhaps, with the dust and blood of the Colosseum, where the roar of the crowd was not merely entertainment but the formalized sound of codified savagery. The spectacle of the arena was the ultimate performance of power, a meticulously staged ballet of death designed to affirm the gods’ order and the brutal hierarchy of the Roman world. Yet, the cruelty of the past, while visceral, often felt distant, mediated by the bronze and the distance of history. Today, the stage has dissolved, migrating from marble floors to flickering screens. We now witness digital violence, the curated horror of propaganda, and the instantaneous, viral dissemination of suffering across fiber optic cables. The difference in medium is negligible; the core mechanism remains identical.

This chapter argues that cruelty is rarely just an act of private malice; it is fundamentally a *performance* designed to structure social reality and define the boundaries between the self and the other. It is a theatrical act, enacted not just by the perpetrator, but by the very structure of the social space itself. Cruelty functions as a social ritual—a necessary, albeit abhorrent, mechanism through which power is asserted, hierarchies are cemented, and shared, often toxic, emotional realities are forged. To understand the architecture of human darkness, we must move beyond judging the act of cruelty in isolation and examine *how* it is staged, witnessed, and consumed. To do this, we will draw upon the frameworks of René Girard, Elaine Scarry, and Judith Butler, exploring how the mechanics of mimetic desire, the phenomenology of pain, and the theory of performativity converge to reveal cruelty as an inescapable feature of human social organization.

## The Scapegoat as Social Catalyst: Mimicking Girardian Desire and Violence

To understand the mobilization of cruelty, one must first understand the psychological engine that drives it: the structure of mimetic desire. René Girard posited that human desire is fundamentally relational; we do not desire objects for their intrinsic qualities alone, but for what they signify in relation to others. This structure establishes a dynamic where competitive desire—the desire for what another possesses—is the engine that drives social conflict. In this system, the self is constantly positioned in relation to the Other, and this relational positioning is inherently unstable, breeding envy and rivalry.

The mechanism of mimetic desire dictates that true satisfaction is unattainable because the object of desire is always already desired by another. This creates an infinite loop of competitive longing, a state of perpetual dissatisfaction that demands resolution. When this tension between internal lack and external possession becomes too acute, it requires a catastrophic intervention. This is where the scapegoat enters the narrative, not as a mere victim, but as the necessary catalyst for the entire social drama.

Cruelty, in this light, is the violent resolution of this mimetic tension. The scapegoat is the figure onto whom the accumulated, unmanageable frustrations of the collective are projected. By designating an external entity as wholly responsible for the community’s ills, the group can momentarily halt the internal spiral of competitive envy. The violence enacted upon the scapegoat becomes a ritualistic purging, a public exorcism that temporarily realigns the social equilibrium by externalizing the unbearable tension. The act of cruelty, therefore, is not arbitrary; it is the violent attempt to manage the social architecture built upon competitive longing.

The transition from private envy to public purge is a crucial step in understanding this mechanism. Before the scapegoat is identified, the conflict remains largely internalized—a simmering resentment, a sense of inadequacy, a corrosive dissatisfaction. This private dissatisfaction, however, is too diffuse and personal to incite large-scale, organized violence. It requires a focal point, a tangible entity onto whom the abstract frustration can be mapped. The scapegoat becomes the external manifestation of the group’s collective failure or their shared, unacknowledged desires.

When the violence is unleashed upon this figure, the act transforms from a private expression of envy into a public performance of collective will. The cruelty serves a dual function: it punishes the scapegoat, thereby satisfying the immediate, visceral demand for retribution, and it reaffirms the boundaries of the in-group by violently demarcating the out-group. The shared experience of witnessing this purging solidifies the group’s internal cohesion, creating a shared, albeit horrific, emotional reality forged in the crucible of shared suffering. Thus, the scapegoat mechanism reveals that cruelty is not merely an aberration of morality, but a deeply embedded, ritualized strategy for managing the volatile dynamics of social comparison and competitive desire.

## The Corporeal Spectacle: Pain, Visibility, and the Creation of Shared Reality

Having established the mechanism by which cruelty is mobilized, we now turn to the experience of that mobilization. If cruelty is a social ritual, its power derives not just from the intention of the actors, but from the way that pain is made visible and embodied. This transition moves us from the abstract structure of social division to the concrete reality of physical suffering, inviting us to the phenomenological realm explored by Elaine Scarry.

For Scarry, particularly in *The Body in Pain*, pain is not merely a private sensation; it is an event that ruptures the ordinary flow of experience and forces a specific mode of perception. When cruelty is enacted, the body becomes the primary site of the performance. The physical agony is immediate, unmediated, and utterly undeniable. It bypasses the slow, rational processes of ethical deliberation and forces the spectator into a raw, immediate emotional state. The sheer visibility of suffering compels an encounter that cannot be ignored; it establishes a non-negotiable emotional truth that supersedes discursive argument.

The body, when subjected to extreme pain, ceases to be a private, interior experience and becomes a public signifier. The spectacle of pain forces the audience into a specific mode of perception—one of immediate, visceral recognition rather than intellectual critique. The horror is not something to be analyzed from a safe distance; it is something to be felt in the visceral gut. This forced immediacy is what allows the spectator to enter the shared reality of the ritual. The pain acts as a bridge, a sensory conduit that bypasses the intellectual defenses that typically guard against contemplating moral transgression.

Furthermore, the aestheticization of suffering is central to this process. The raw, chaotic reality of physical agony is immediately transformed into a legible, consumable spectacle. The audience does not merely observe suffering; they consume it as a spectacle. This transformation is not a softening of the horror, but an amplification of its communicative power. Raw pain, in its unfiltered state, can be paralyzing; when framed within a ritual, it becomes legible, structured, and therefore, meaningful. The spectacle allows the audience to process immense, chaotic suffering not as an overwhelming threat to their rational composure, but as a structured, albeit horrific, event within a known, if dark, framework.

This spectacle functions to create a shared reality. By witnessing the corporeal reality of suffering, participants—both the perpetrators and the spectators—are forced into a unified phenomenological space. The pain becomes a common denominator, a shared emotional terrain upon which the social structure is momentarily, violently reaffirmed. It is in this shared, embodied experience that the boundaries between the self and the other are brutally clarified, and the social roles—victim and perpetrator, observer and witnessed—are inscribed onto the very fabric of the experience.

## Performing the Hierarchy: Gender, Power, and the Performativity of Cruelty

If the scapegoat mechanism explains *why* cruelty is mobilized, and the corporeal spectacle explains *how* it is experienced, the framework of Judith Butler’s performativity illuminates *who* performs it and *what* social structures it solidifies. Cruelty is not merely a reaction to social tension; it is an active, ongoing mode of self-fashioning, a way of enacting and solidifying existing power hierarchies through repeated, stylized acts.

Butler’s concept of performativity suggests that gender, identity, and social roles are not inherent essences but are rather effects produced by the ceaseless repetition of stylized acts. Cruelty, when viewed through this lens, becomes a potent mode of enacted identity. It is a performance where the roles of oppressor and oppressed are not merely roles one inhabits, but roles that are physically and socially materialized through the deployment of violence. The cruelty enacted by the dominant group—whether in the execution of a state-sanctioned order or the daily subjugation of another—is a deliberate performance designed to solidify the inherent rightness of the hierarchy.

This performance operates by policing the gaze. The act of witnessing and recording cruelty is itself a critical component of the performance. The spectacle demands a specific orientation from the audience: they must watch, they must register, and they must categorize the scene according to the established hierarchy. Victims are transformed from subjects with inherent moral claims into passive objects for public scrutiny, their suffering becoming the evidence of the oppressor’s authority. This policing of the gaze ensures that the dominant perspective remains the dominant reality, turning the marginalized into legible markers of difference.

The ritualization of difference is central to this performative power. Cruelty functions to violently delineate the boundaries between the in-group and the out-group. By inflicting suffering upon the scapegoat, the community performs its identity. The act is a public declaration: "We are *this* group, and they are *that* group." This violent demarcation is essential for maintaining the internal coherence of the in-group. The ritual ensures that the boundaries are not just conceptual but are etched into the shared emotional and physical memory of the community. The performance of cruelty, therefore, functions as a tool for social cohesion, solidifying the internal bonds of the group by violently externalizing the perceived threat or difference.

The combination of these lenses reveals a unified social function: cruelty is the means by which power is not just held, but actively demonstrated and sustained. It is the ritualistic enactment of a hierarchy, experienced through embodied pain, and perpetually reinforced by the gaze of the spectators. The performance of cruelty is the mechanism by which social order is made visible, tangible, and emotionally binding.

## The Infinite Stage: Cruelty in the Digital Age and the Economy of Trauma

The mechanisms described—the need for scapegoating, the visceral reality of pain, and the performative construction of hierarchy—were potent in the physical, ritualistic arenas of history. However, the advent of the digital age has introduced a new dimension to the performance of cruelty: the infinite stage. The transition from physical spectacle to mediated spectacle has radically altered the speed, reach, and permanence of suffering, demonstrating that the dynamic of cruelty is not merely historical but infrastructural.

Digital platforms have created an arena where the ritual of cruelty is no longer confined by physical space or temporal limits. The speed at which images and videos of violence—such as the horrific documentation of conflicts or the viral spread of propaganda—can be disseminated is exponential. The physical constraints of the arena are replaced by the boundless reach of the internet, allowing suffering to become instantly and globally visible. The physical pain of the body is sublimated into the digital stream, transforming raw experience into consumable data.

This mediated spectacle fundamentally changes the role of the audience. In the physical arena, the spectator was a bounded witness; in the digital arena, the audience becomes a co-performer. Digital platforms enable passive consumption on a massive scale, yet they also facilitate instantaneous participation. Users are no longer relegated to the role of detached observers; they are integrated into the ritual by liking, sharing, commenting, and engaging with the trauma. This blurring of the line between witness and participant means that the emotional reality forged by the spectacle is no longer solely the domain of the enacted event but is actively co-constructed by the networked audience.

Furthermore, the infrastructure of outrage is inextricably linked to the economy of trauma. Algorithms and media systems are designed to prioritize engagement, and emotional intensity—especially outrage and fear—is the most potent currency for engagement. Cruelty, when performed digitally, is perfectly optimized for this economy. It is a self-perpetuating cycle where the continuous performance of horrific acts generates the attention necessary to sustain the platforms that distribute them. The system profits from the perpetual circulation of pain, using it to maintain social polarization and enforce the boundaries of the in-group by constantly signaling the existence of the out-group as an existential threat.

The digital stage allows for a constant, iterative performance of hierarchy. Propaganda, for instance, is a masterclass in this. It does not merely depict oppression; it performs the *necessity* of oppression, creating a shared, emotionally charged reality where the dominant group’s actions are framed as defensive or righteous. This digital performance bypasses the slow, rational critique that might arise from a physical confrontation, instead embedding the ideology directly into the shared emotional experience of the audience.

In synthesizing these observations, the philosophical implication is profound: cruelty is not an aberration that exists outside the moral order; it is a necessary, if terrifying, mechanism *within* the very architecture of human social relations. Whether staged in the sand of the Colosseum or broadcast across a fiber optic cable, the function of cruelty remains consistent: to structure desire, define boundaries, and create shared, often brutal, emotional realities.
