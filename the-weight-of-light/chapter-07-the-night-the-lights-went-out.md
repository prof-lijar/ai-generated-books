# Chapter 7: The Night the Lights Went Out

The *tasca* was bathed in a specific kind of amber light that seemed unique to Lisbon—a warm, honeyed glow that seeped from the limestone walls and settled into the grain of the weathered wooden tables. It was a small, crowded sanctuary smelling of grilled sardines, garlic, and old wine. Around them, the hum of conversation formed a comfortable tapestry: the rhythmic clink of glasses, the melodic murmur of Portuguese vowels, and the distant laughter of regulars who had occupied these stools for decades.

Mira sat across from Jonah, her eyes tracing the way the light caught the condensation on their water glasses. For the last hour, she had been "painting with words," a game they played to bridge the gap between his world and hers. She described the vibrant saffron of a woman’s silk scarf—a color that seemed to vibrate against the muted grey of her coat—and the deep, bruised purple of the twilight spilling through the open doorway.

"It's not just purple," Mira whispered, leaning in closer. "It's like an ink-wash painting. The edges are soft, bleeding into a charcoal blue where the streetlights haven’t quite taken hold yet."

Jonah smiled, his sightless eyes focused somewhere near her voice. He listened with a concentrated intensity that always made Mira feel as though she were being seen more clearly than anyone had ever truly looked at her. To him, these descriptions weren't just information; they were architecture. She was building a world for him in real-time, and in return, he offered her a stillness she hadn’t known since before the wars.

Then, the light vanished.

There was no flicker, no warning dimming of the bulbs. In one heartbeat, the room was golden and alive; in the next, there was an absolute, crushing void. 

The transition was so violent that Mira felt a physical jolt in her chest. The lights didn't just go out—they were stolen, taking with them every point of reference she possessed. A collective gasp rippled through the *tasca*, followed by a sudden, jarring silence as electronics died and conversations froze mid-sentence. Then came the disorientation: chairs scraping against tile, a glass shattering on the floor, and voices rising in pitch, asking what had happened.

In the center of this burgeoning chaos, Mira froze. Her heart hammered against her ribs like a trapped bird. She felt the air thicken, pressing against her skin until each breath became an effort. 

Beside her, Jonah remained perfectly still. While the sighted patrons were stumbling and blinking, disoriented by the sudden theft of their vision, Jonah hadn't moved an inch. To him, the world had not fundamentally changed. The table was still beneath his fingers; Mira’s scent—jasmine and a faint hint of darkroom chemicals—was still there; the layout of the room remained constant. He was the only person in the room who wasn't lost.

But for Mira, the darkness wasn't merely an absence of light. It was a door that had swung open, admitting everything she had spent years trying to lock away.

As panic surged, the boundaries of the *tasca* began to blur. Mira’s breathing became shallow and jagged, each inhale feeling as though it were being filtered through sand. She wasn't in Lisbon anymore; she was drifting backward through time, pulled by a tide of sensory triggers that her mind could no longer ignore.

In the blackout, silence ceased to be peaceful—it became predatory. The distant sound of a heavy car door slamming outside didn’t register as traffic; it echoed in her skull as the concussive blast of an IED. She flinched violently, her shoulders hunching toward her ears, expecting the rain of debris and the scream of sirens that always followed such a noise.

The wind whistling through the narrow alleyways began to sound like whispering warnings—voices of people she had failed to save, calling out from the ruins of cities whose names she tried not to speak aloud. 

Mira’s mind became a projector, casting high-definition images onto the black canvas of her surroundings. For years, as a war photographer, she had captured horror in crisp detail: the stark white of bone against grey rubble, the iridescent sheen of oil on bloodied pavement, the hollow stare of children who had seen too much. She had mastered the art of framing these tragedies through a lens to keep them at a distance, but without the lens—and without the light—the images flooded her consciousness unfiltered and overwhelming.

The darkness was no longer an empty space; it was crowded with ghosts. 

A wave of claustrophobia washed over her despite being in an open room. It felt as if the walls were shrinking, trapping her in a cellar or a collapsed building. Her hands trembled uncontrollably, gripping the edge of the table so hard that her knuckles turned white—though there was no light to see them. She wanted to scream, but her throat had tightened into a narrow straw, allowing only thin, wheezing gasps of air to escape.

"Mira."

The voice wasn't loud or demanding. It was a steady, low frequency that sliced through the noise in her head like a beacon cutting through fog. 

Jonah didn’t reach for her immediately; he knew enough about panic to know that an unexpected touch could feel like an assault when someone is spiraling. Instead, he used his voice as a tether, anchoring her to the present moment with the sheer consistency of its tone.

"Listen to me," he said softly. "You're in Lisbon. You are at the table. I am right here."

Mira couldn’t answer. She was drowning in sensory distortion, the ghost-sounds of war overlapping with the confused chatter of the tavern. 

"I want you to find one sound," Jonah continued, his voice calm and rhythmic. "Just one. Listen past the people. Listen for the clock on the wall."

Mira strained her ears. At first, she heard only the roar of blood in her temples, but then, beneath it all, there was a faint, mechanical *tick-tock*. It was slow, predictable, and real. 

"Good," Jonah whispered. "Now listen for my watch. The windup one."

She focused harder. There it was—a faster, thinner metallic clicking coming from his wrist. Two different rhythms, two distinct points of reality. They were small things, insignificant to anyone else, but to Mira, they were coordinates on a map leading her back to the surface.

Then, he reached out and took her hand. 

The shift in power was instantaneous. For months, Mira had been Jonah’s guide, translating the visual world into language for him. She had felt a protective instinct toward him, a desire to shield him from a city designed for the sighted. But now, as his fingers locked around hers, she realized that he was the one holding her together. 

His grip was steady and sure—a physical manifestation of stability. His skin was warm, providing a tangible boundary between her mind and the void. It wasn't just guidance; it was a transfer of strength. In the absolute darkness, Mira had become the blind one, and Jonah had become the sight.

"I have you," he said, and for the first time in years, she believed that someone actually did.

"We can't stay here," Jonah murmured, sensing the rising agitation of the crowd as people began to push toward the exits. "Let’s walk. Just follow me."

As they stepped out of the *tasca* and into the streets of Lisbon, Mira felt a fresh surge of terror. The city had become an abyss. Every cobblestone felt like a precipice; every shadow seemed to reach for her ankles. She clung to Jonah's hand with a desperation that bordered on pain, her body leaning heavily into his.

"Close your eyes," Jonah told her. 

Mira blinked in confusion. "What? It’s already dark."

"Close them anyway," he insisted gently. "Stop trying to see what isn't there. Stop fighting the darkness and start listening to it. Let me show you how I see the city."

Slowly, Mira obeyed. By shutting her eyes, she stopped the frantic, useless searching of her pupils. She surrendered her visual expectation and leaned entirely into Jonah’s perception. 

As they walked, Jonah began to narrate a world that existed beyond light. He pointed out "the music" of the city—the way their footsteps changed tone as they moved from an open square onto narrow limestone paths. 

"Listen," he whispered. "Hear how the sound bounces off these walls? We're in a corridor now. The air is denser here, cooler. Can you feel it on your skin?"

Mira focused. She noticed the subtle change in air pressure as they passed an open doorway—a sudden slipstream of cool air that smelled of old paper and beeswax. Then came the scent of salt, heavy and brine-thick, drifting up from the Tagus River a few blocks away. 

The city began to resolve itself into a series of echoes and textures. She heard the syncopation of high heels on cobblestones—a sharp *clack-clack* that told her exactly where other pedestrians were moving. From a distance, she caught the haunting melody of a Fado house; someone was singing by candlelight, their voice raw and longing, weaving through the dark alleys like a ribbon of smoke.

The climax of this trust exercise came when they reached one of Lisbon's narrowest alleyways—a passage so tight that if they both put out their arms, they could nearly touch opposite walls. For Mira, it felt as though she were being swallowed by the earth. Her breath hitched and her pace slowed to a crawl.

"Trust me," Jonah said, his voice right beside her ear. "Just one step at a time."

She let go of the tension in her shoulders and allowed him to pull her forward. She stopped calculating the distance; she stopped imagining pitfalls. She simply followed the rhythm of his stride and the warmth of his hand. In that narrow space, surrounded by oppressive blackness, Mira felt a strange sense of liberation. By surrendering completely to Jonah's lead, she was escaping the prison of her own memories. For once, she didn’t have to be the one in control; she didn't have to be the observer. She could simply *be*.

They eventually reached the sanctuary of his apartment, a small space filled with the scent of cedar and tea. As they stepped inside and closed the door, the silence that followed was no longer threatening—it was intimate. 

The blackout persisted, leaving the rooms in shadow, but Mira didn't panic this time. She sank into one of the oversized velvet chairs, her body finally giving way to a profound exhaustion. Jonah moved through the room with an effortless grace, lighting several thick beeswax candles that cast flickering shadows across the walls.

They sat together in the soft light, the distance between them vanished. 

"Why does it scare you so much?" Jonah asked softly. "The dark."

Mira looked at her hands, which were finally still. "In my work... and before that... the darkness was where things happened," she whispered, her voice trembling. "It's where people hide when they’re hurting. It's where you wait for a bomb to go off because you can hear it coming but you can't see where it is. For me, light isn't just visibility; it's safety. When the lights went out tonight, I felt like all my defenses had been stripped away."

Jonah was silent for a moment, then he leaned back and looked up at the ceiling, though his eyes saw only black. 

"For me," he said, "darkness isn't an absence of anything. It’s just another way of experiencing things. I have this... thing with sound. Synesthesia, they call it. When you speak, or when the wind hits the eaves of the house, I don't just hear it. I see colors. Your voice is a deep, shimmering gold—like that light you described in the *tasca*. The Fado singer we heard? She was a wash of indigo and silver."

Mira looked at him with wonder. "You see colors in sounds?"

"I do," he smiled. "So for me, there is no such thing as 'the dark.' There's just a different kind of light. A light that doesn’t come from the sun or a bulb, but from the world itself."

Mira felt a lump form in her throat. She realized then that they were both carrying burdens—hers born of traumatic memory, his born of biology. They had spent their lives navigating different versions of darkness, and for so long, she had viewed him as someone who needed protection. But tonight, he had been the one to rescue her from herself.

As the hours passed, neither of them mentioned going back to their separate apartments. The city outside remained plunged in shadow, but inside the small circle of candlelight, the weight of the world felt lighter. 

Mira didn't want to leave. She realized that she no longer feared the void because she was no longer alone within it. As they eventually lay down together, wrapped in a shared blanket and listening to the distant hum of a city slowly waking up from its slumber, Mira closed her eyes. For the first time in years, she didn't see ghosts when she did so; she only felt the steady, rhythmic beat of Jonah’s heart against hers—a light that no blackout could ever extinguish.
